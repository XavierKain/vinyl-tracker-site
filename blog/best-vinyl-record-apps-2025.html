The vinyl revival isn't slowing down. Record sales have grown for 18 consecutive years, and with more collectors than ever, the demand for good vinyl collection management apps has never been higher. But with so many options available, finding the right one can be overwhelming.

We spent three weeks testing seven of the most popular vinyl record apps available in 2025. We cataloged the same 200-record collection in each app, evaluated their features, and assessed everything from barcode scanning accuracy to export flexibility. Here's what we found.

Whether you're a beginner with 30 records or a serious collector with thousands, this guide will help you find the right tool for your needs and budget. If you're brand new to collecting and need more foundational advice, start with our beginner's guide to vinyl collecting.

What to Look for in a Vinyl Record App

Before diving into specific apps, let's establish what actually matters in a vinyl collection manager. These are the criteria we used to evaluate each app:

Barcode scanning. The ability to scan a record's UPC/EAN barcode and automatically populate album data is the single most important time-saving feature. Without it, you're stuck typing everything manually. We tested scanning accuracy and speed across all apps.

Database quality. The barcode scanner is only as good as the database behind it. We looked at which databases each app uses (MusicBrainz, Discogs, proprietary) and how complete and accurate the data is, especially for older, rarer, or international releases.

Offline access. Can you browse and search your collection without internet? For many collectors, this is critical — you want to check your catalog while browsing at a record shop, flea market, or estate sale where Wi-Fi might not be available.

Data ownership and export. Your collection data should be yours. We checked whether each app lets you export your data in standard formats (CSV, JSON) and whether your data is stored locally or only on the developer's servers.

Pricing model. Subscription fatigue is real. We compared one-time purchases, subscriptions, freemium models, and truly free options. The total cost of ownership over 3 years varies dramatically.

User experience. How does it feel to actually use the app? Is it fast? Is the interface clean or cluttered? Can you find a specific record in your 1,000-record collection quickly? We paid close attention to search speed, navigation, and visual design.

Statistics and insights. Beyond basic cataloging, does the app help you understand your collection? Genre breakdowns, decade distributions, artist counts, and collection value estimates are all valuable features for serious collectors.

1. Vinyl Tracker — Best Overall

Price: $4.99 one-time
Platform: Web (PWA — works on all devices)
Database: MusicBrainz
Offline: Yes (full offline access)
Export: CSV, JSON

Vinyl Tracker is a progressive web app built specifically for personal vinyl collection management. It does one thing and does it extremely well: let you catalog your records quickly, browse your collection offline, and never pay a recurring fee.

The barcode scanning is fast and accurate, powered by MusicBrainz's database of over 50 million releases. In our testing, it correctly identified 187 out of 200 records from barcode alone — the remaining 13 were pre-barcode era records that required manual search, which was also quick and easy.

What sets Vinyl Tracker apart is its offline-first architecture. Your entire collection is stored locally using IndexedDB and localStorage. This means the app loads instantly, searches are near-instantaneous regardless of collection size, and you can browse your catalog anywhere — no internet required. For collectors who frequently shop at flea markets, garage sales, and record fairs, this is a game-changer.

The statistics dashboard provides a clear overview of your collection: total records, genre breakdown, decade distribution, top artists, and format types. It's not as deep as some paid alternatives, but it covers what most collectors actually want to know.

The pricing is refreshingly simple: $4.99 once, everything included, no subscription, no ads, no upsells. Over three years, that's $4.99 total compared to $89.64 for a CLZ subscription.

Pros: Fast barcode scanning, full offline access, one-time pricing, clean interface, MusicBrainz integration, data stays on your device, export to CSV/JSON.

Cons: No marketplace features, no social/community features, relatively new (smaller user community), no desktop-specific app (PWA works in browser).

Best for: Collectors who want a fast, private, no-nonsense catalog without recurring costs.

2. Discogs — Best for Marketplace Integration

Price: Free (with ads and marketplace fees)
Platform: Web, iOS, Android
Database: Discogs (proprietary, community-maintained)
Offline: Limited
Export: CSV

Discogs is the 800-pound gorilla of vinyl collection management. With over 15 million releases in its database and a thriving marketplace, it's the default choice for many collectors. And for good reason — the database is enormous, the community is active, and the basic collection features are free.

The Discogs database is arguably the most comprehensive record database in the world. It's community-maintained by over 700,000 contributors, and it includes incredibly detailed information about pressings, variants, and releases that no other database matches. If you own a rare Japanese pressing of a 1970s jazz album, Discogs almost certainly has it cataloged.

However, Discogs has significant drawbacks as a pure collection management tool. The web interface is cluttered with marketplace features, seller listings, and ads (unless you pay for a Discogs Gold subscription at $2/month). The mobile app is functional but not particularly fast or polished. And most critically, you need internet access to use it — there's no meaningful offline mode.

The search and browsing experience for your own collection is adequate but not great. As your collection grows into the hundreds or thousands, finding a specific record can feel sluggish compared to locally-stored alternatives. The statistics page provides basic counts but lacks the visual polish and depth of dedicated apps.

Pros: Massive database, marketplace integration, free, strong community, detailed pressing info.

Cons: Requires internet, cluttered interface, ads on free tier, slow mobile experience, data lives on their servers, no barcode scanning in web app.

Best for: Collectors who actively buy and sell records and want marketplace integration with their catalog.

3. CLZ Music — Best for Power Users

Price: $2.49/month or $24.95/year
Platform: iOS, Android, Web
Database: CLZ Core (proprietary)
Offline: Yes (synced locally)
Export: CSV, XML

CLZ Music (formerly Collectorz.com Music Collector Mobile) is the Swiss Army knife of collection management apps. It's feature-rich, highly customizable, and has been around for over a decade, which means it's battle-tested with large collections.

The barcode scanning works well and uses CLZ's proprietary "Core" database, which is maintained by a dedicated team rather than community volunteers. This means data quality is generally high, though the database is smaller than Discogs or MusicBrainz for very obscure releases.

CLZ excels at customization. You can add custom fields, create custom sorting rules, and configure the interface to show exactly what you want. The statistics are comprehensive, and the app handles collections of 10,000+ records without breaking a sweat.

The main drawback is the subscription model. At $2.49/month, it's not expensive in isolation, but it adds up — $89.64 over three years. And if you stop paying, you lose access to the scanning and cloud sync features. Your data is technically still accessible but the app becomes significantly less useful. For a collection management tool that's essentially a local database with a scanner, a subscription feels aggressive.

Pros: Feature-rich, highly customizable, good scanner, handles large collections, reliable company.

Cons: Subscription required, proprietary database (less comprehensive for niche releases), interface feels dated, cloud sync can be slow.

Best for: Power users with large collections who want maximum customization and don't mind paying monthly.

4. Vinyl Scrobbler — Best for Last.fm Users

Price: Free
Platform: iOS, Android
Database: Discogs
Offline: No
Export: No

Vinyl Scrobbler occupies a unique niche — it's primarily a tool for scrobbling your vinyl listening to Last.fm, with collection management as a secondary feature. If you're a Last.fm user who wants to track your vinyl listening alongside your digital music, this is your best option.

The app connects to your Discogs collection and lets you "play" a record to scrobble its tracks to Last.fm. It's simple, focused, and does its specific job well. But as a collection manager, it's limited — you're essentially using Discogs for the catalog and Vinyl Scrobbler as a listening log.

Pros: Last.fm integration, free, simple to use.

Cons: Not a real collection manager, requires Discogs account, no offline mode, no export, limited features.

Best for: Last.fm users who want to scrobble vinyl listening sessions.

5. Collectorz.com Music — Best Desktop App

Price: $29.95 one-time (desktop) + optional $2.49/month for mobile
Platform: Windows, macOS
Database: CLZ Core
Offline: Yes
Export: CSV, XML, HTML

The desktop sibling of CLZ Music, Collectorz.com Music Collector is a full-featured desktop application for serious catalogers. If you prefer working on a computer rather than a phone, and you have a large collection to manage, this is worth considering.

The desktop app is faster than the mobile version and offers more screen real estate for browsing large collections. The barcode scanner works with a USB barcode reader or webcam, and the software supports batch scanning for rapidly cataloging large collections. You can process a box of 100 records in under an hour.

The export options are excellent — CSV, XML, and even formatted HTML reports. The database is the same CLZ Core database used by the mobile app, with the same strengths and limitations.

Pros: Powerful desktop experience, batch scanning, extensive export options, one-time purchase (desktop only), handles massive collections.

Cons: Desktop-only (mobile requires separate subscription), dated interface design, Windows/macOS only, no web access.

Best for: Collectors who prefer desktop software and want to batch-catalog large collections.

6. MyMusicQ — Best Free Option

Price: Free
Platform: iOS, Android
Database: MusicBrainz, Discogs
Offline: Partial
Export: CSV

MyMusicQ is a free, ad-supported vinyl collection app that punches above its weight. It uses both MusicBrainz and Discogs as data sources, giving it broad coverage, and it includes basic barcode scanning in the free tier.

The interface is clean and functional, though not as polished as premium alternatives. The ads are unobtrusive — mostly banner ads at the bottom of the screen — and they don't interfere with core functionality. Collection statistics are basic but present.

The biggest limitation is partial offline access. Your collection data is cached locally, but search and new additions require internet. This makes it less useful for on-the-go catalog checks at record shops with poor cell coverage.

Pros: Free, dual database sources, barcode scanning included, clean interface, CSV export.

Cons: Ads, limited offline access, basic statistics, occasional sync issues, smaller user community.

Best for: Budget-conscious collectors who want a decent free app and don't mind occasional ads.

7. Google Sheets — Best for Total Control

Price: Free
Platform: Web, iOS, Android
Database: None (manual entry)
Offline: Yes (with Google Sheets offline mode)
Export: CSV, XLSX, and more

Don't overlook the humble spreadsheet. Google Sheets (or Excel, or LibreOffice Calc) gives you complete control over your catalog structure with zero lock-in. You define the columns, the formatting, the sorting — everything.

The obvious downside is that there's no barcode scanning, no automatic metadata lookup, and no cover art integration. Every record must be entered manually. For a collection of 50 records, this is fine. For 500+, it becomes tedious enough that most collectors abandon the effort.

That said, spreadsheets have some unique advantages. You can create complex formulas for value calculations, use conditional formatting to highlight gaps in your collection, and share your sheet with anyone. The data format is universally readable, and you'll never worry about an app being discontinued.

For a guide on setting up an effective cataloging system, see our article on how to catalog your vinyl collection.

Pros: Free, total flexibility, no lock-in, works everywhere, formulas and formatting.

Cons: No barcode scanning, no auto-metadata, manual data entry, no cover art, no statistics, poor mobile browsing experience.

Best for: Very small collections, people who love spreadsheets, or as a backup/export format.

Which App Should You Choose?

Choosing the right app depends on what you value most. Here's our recommendation framework:

If you want the best balance of features and value: Vinyl Tracker. One-time price, full offline access, fast scanning, clean interface. It does everything most collectors need without the bloat or recurring costs.

If you buy and sell records regularly: Discogs. The marketplace integration is unmatched, and having your catalog tied to the world's largest record marketplace is genuinely useful for sellers.

If you have 5,000+ records and need heavy customization: CLZ Music or Collectorz Desktop. These are the power tools of the vinyl cataloging world, built for serious collectors who need maximum flexibility.

If you want to scrobble vinyl to Last.fm: Vinyl Scrobbler. It's the only app designed for this specific use case.

If you want free and don't mind ads: MyMusicQ. A solid free option that covers the basics well.

If you have a tiny collection or love spreadsheets: Google Sheets. Complete control, zero cost, universal compatibility.

Final Verdict

The vinyl record app market in 2025 is mature and competitive, which is great news for collectors. There's genuinely no bad option on this list — each app serves its audience well.

That said, for most collectors — the person with a growing collection who wants to catalog efficiently, browse offline, and not pay monthly for the privilege — Vinyl Tracker represents the best value proposition in the market today. Fast scanning, MusicBrainz's massive database, true offline access, and a one-time $4.99 price make it hard to beat.
